Research

Understanding immune surveillance mechanisms to develop next-generation cancer immunotherapies

Research Overview

Our central research interest is understanding the fundamental principles that determine the strength and consequence of immune surveillance during tumorigenesis and therapy. Our short-term objective is to elucidate how mutagenesis processes and DNA damage responses impact immune recognition and escape, particularly in hypermutated and DDR-deficient tumors. Our long-term research objective is to leverage such information to develop next-generation immunotherapies for more efficient cancer prevention and treatment.

Mismatch Repair & Homologous Recombination Deficiencies

Deciphering how BRCA1/BRCA2 mutations and MMR deficiencies differentially affect the tumor microenvironment and response to checkpoint blockade immunotherapy. We investigate the molecular mechanisms underlying enhanced immunogenicity in DNA repair-deficient tumors.

Key Techniques:

  • Immunogenomic analysis of patient cohorts
  • Tumor microenvironment characterization

EGFR T790M Resistance Targeting

Developing novel T cell receptor agents to target the EGFR T790M resistance mutation in non-small cell lung cancer through innovative immunotherapy approaches. This work is supported by the Caregiver Catalyst Grant from Cleveland Clinic Foundation.

POLE/POLD1 Functional Landscapes

Hypoalbuminemia & Immunotherapy

Investigating the metabolic and pharmacological consequences of low serum albumin and its impact on immune checkpoint blockade efficacy. Understanding how systemic factors affect immunotherapy outcomes.
